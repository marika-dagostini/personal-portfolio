About me

I'm a PhD student in Statistical Sciences at the University of Bologna, where I work with Professor Massimo Ventrucci and Professor Andrea Ranzi. Alongside research, I also work as a teaching tutor in Bayesian Inference.

The PhD scholarship is funded by ARPAE (Regional Agency for Prevention, Environment and Energy in Emilia-Romagna), within the project “Health and Equity Co-Benefits in Support of Plans to Respond to Climate Change in Italy”, part of the Italian National Plan for Complementary Investments of the Ministry of Health.

My main research interests include Bayesian hierarchical models, disease mapping, and latent factor models, with applications in environmental and epidemiological studies. You can find more details and related projects across this website.

Research Interests

  • Spatial & Spatio-Temporal Bayesian Hierarchical Models

    Bayesian hierarchical modelling for spatial and spatio-temporal data, with a focus on INLA-based inference and applications in environmental and epidemiological settings where traditional MCMC approaches can be computationally demanding.

  • Urban Heat Islands

    Using high-resolution remote sensing data and Bayesian hierarchical models to study environmental exposures in epidemiological research, with a particular interest in urban heat islands, the evolution of thermal patterns in cities, and the role of land cover and vegetation in shaping thermal discomfort.

  • Urban Vulnerability Indicators

    Spatial Bayesian latent factor models to construct composite indicators of climate and environmental vulnerability in urban areas, integrating multiple data sources and quantifying uncertainty in a coherent probabilistic framework.

  • Disease Mapping

    Bayesian disease mapping in settings where data are aggregated over small or irregular spatial units, and differences in population size and spatial structure can make standard approaches unstable or misleading.

  • Data Visualization

    Turning complex spatial analyses into web-based mapping applications and interactive visualizations that make results easier to explore, interpret, and use for decision-making.

  • Knitting

    Perhaps not (yet) a formal academic discipline, but one with more maths and trial-and-error research than one might expect... somewhere between statistical modelling and creative debugging.
